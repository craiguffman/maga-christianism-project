# Jenkins.J._KFTA_06:  225

As Aquinas says, "to command is an act of reason, although one presupposing an act of will; in the power of this [act of will], reason moves one to act through its command."71 That is, although it is by one's will that something is commanded, it is one's reason which intimates what is to be commanded.72 Thus, if the will is to command, there must be some act of reason by which the content of the command is intimated. But one's reason must intimate not only an end to be pursued (e.g. eternal life), but also the action or actions which are the preferred means to this end (e.g. assenting firmly to the articles of the Christian Creed).Read more at location 2507
