The Metrics We Inherit

We inherit metrics before we choose them. My father, a proud businessman, tracked his success in promotions and salary increases. My mother, though never employed outside our home, measured her days in meals prepared, surfaces cleaned, children raised. Their Depression-era upbringing made productivity the ultimate virtue, idleness the unforgivable sin.

The metrics that matter in perennial living are different:

Learning New Measurements

Retraining myself to value these different metrics hasn't been easy. I still find myself instinctively counting, achieving, producing. But slowly, I'm learning to measure what truly matters:

From productivity to fruitfulness. Productivity measures output regardless of meaning; fruitfulness measures output that brings life to others and glory to God.

This shift has been particularly important in my health journey. I no longer judge a workout by calories burned or miles covered, but by how it connected me to my body, how it fostered gratitude for movement, how it built sustainable strength rather than impressive performance.

The Liberation of Better Metrics

There's a profound liberation in measuring what truly matters. When productivity is your primary metric, rest feels like failure. When achievement drives your worth, limitation feels like punishment. When comparison determines your value, community becomes competition.

But when your metrics shift toward rootedness, presence, integration, and surrender, everything changes. Rest becomes essential rather than indulgent. Limitation becomes invitation rather than obstacle. Community becomes communion rather than competition.
